Background¶

The National Basketball Association (NBA) is the premier basketball league for North America. It is widely regarded as the most competitive league in the world. Play consists of two teams, each with five players on the court at all times. Traditionally, a five-man lineup would consist of five unique positions: a point guard (PG), a shooting guard (SG), a small forward (SF), a power forward (PF), and a center (C). Each of these positions performs a unique role on the team, albiet with some overlap between positions. For example, a point guard traditionally runs the team's offense (handles the ball, calls plays to run, excels in passing the ball, etc).

Problem¶

Can one predict the position a NBA player plays based on their box-score statistics? Or in other words, does a player's performace indicate the position they play?

Significance¶

This is an incredibly important scouting problem for NBA teams. Often, the transition from amateur basketball, college basketball, or other professional leagues is wrought with missclassification or missidentification of talent. NBA scouts will try to fill gaps in their roster by acquiring players from college teams (through the NBA draft) or signing amateur/professional players from other basketball leagues. However, as stated above, the level of competition in the NBA is unparalleled, and as such the role a college or semi-pro player plays on their original team will likely not be the same role they play in the NBA. Creating a classification system which identifies the position a player plays based on their statistics would mitigate the risks associated with acquiring players from leagues outside of the NBA. Likewise, once a player arrives in the NBA, a predictive model could help identify if coaches are playing a player at the wrong position based on the performance of that player.

Dataset¶

Our dataset contains box-score statistics on every NBA player for every NBA season from 1950 to 2017. There are 24,630 records, each with 54 attributes. These attributes include basic counting stats such as points, rebounds, assists, steals, and blocks, as well as advanced statistics such as true-shooting percentage. All of the 54 attributes are described in the notebook glossary. Each record in the dataset is a single player's statistics for a single season.

We could expect each of the traditional positions to have a general trend in their box-score statistics.

  • Point Guard: The player to normally bring the ball down and start each play. We could see increased numbers in assists and usage since they will be the main ball handler. Also, the number of steals they have could be higher compared to the other positions because they are the quickest.
  • Shooting Guard: Shooting guards and small forwards are usually referred to as "wings" because of their positioning: they tend to hover further away the basket, typically around the 3 point line. One of the main objectives for the shooting guard is to score points for their team, so they typically will have a higher total points statline. They also act as secondary ball handlers.
  • Small Forward: Small forwards tend to be the most versatile players on the court. They tend to have a well-rounded offensive skillset and can defend well because of their length. Therefore, we would expect a more even statline because small forwards have the skills to perform many roles.
  • Power Forward: As these players tend to stay closer to the rim than the other positions (besides the center), we could expect to see increased blocks, rebounds, and 2 point field goal percentage since they tend to be bigger and play closer to rim.
  • Center: The player who is normally the biggest and tallest on the court, so we could expect see large increases in blocks and rebounds and a small increase in FG% (shooting closest to rim).

The # attribute in our dataset was a unique identifier for each player's season. Instead of using this unique identifier, we will use index assigned by pandas. As the blank2 and blanl were placeholders for the original aggregator of the dataset, we will also drop them from our dataframe.

We also only want to consider seasons after the introduction of the 3-point shot into the NBA. The 3-point shot was introduced in the 1979-1980 NBA season which is considered the start of the "modern era" of basketball. After the introduction of the 3-point shot, the frontcourt (SF, PF, and C) and backcourt (PG, SG) started to define their roles more distinctly, with guards and small forwards tending to have better 3-point shooting capabilities than the other positions.

Players with less than 311 minutes of gametime simply do not record enough statistics to be significant because they will be playing fewer than 7 minutes per game.

Feature Engineering¶

(Possiblities: Scale all stats by minutes, scale per game?, give some features more weight than others)

(Possible reasons why models can be wrong: Backup players having different stats compared to starters, some players that could play "outside" their position, positions responsibilities change over time, positionless statistics, )

There are a variety of "positionless" statistics that players record regardless of the position they play. In our case, these include:

  • WS, WS/48, OWS, DWS: Win Shares; metrics designed to estimate the number of wins a player contributes to their team's total wins
  • G, GS: Games and games started; number of games a player played in that season and the number of games a player started that season, respectively
  • PF: Personal Fouls; the number of fouls commited by a player during the season
  • PER: Player Efficiency Rating; a rating indicating a player's per-minute performance while on the court
  • MP: Minutes Played; number of minutes a player plays that season)
  • BPM, OBPM, and DBPM: Box plus-minus; box score-based metric that estimates a basketball players offensive contribution to the team when that player is on the court
  • VORP: Value over Replacement Player; an estimate of each player's contribution to their team relative to a "replacement-level" player

Finally, there are some attributes that are derived from others in the dataset. To decrease redundancy and dimensionality, we eliminated these from our dataset.

  • 3P% = 3P / 3PA
  • 2P% = 2P / 2PA
  • FG% = FG / FGA
  • FT% = FT / FTA
  • FTr = FTA / FGA
  • eFG% = (FG + .5 * 3P) / FGA
  • FG = 2P + 3P
  • TRB = ORB + DRB
  • PTS = 3 3P + 2 2P + FT
  • FGA = 3PA + 2PA
  • 3PAr = 3PA / FGA
  • TS% = PTS / ( 2 (FGA + 0.44 FTA))

One possible issue with the current data is stat inflation. Players stats scale with the amount of playing time they get (MP). Because the percentages are already scaled, we will be scaling 3P, 3PA, 2P, 2PA FT, FTA, ORB, DRB, AST, STL, BLK, TOV by dividing all of those numbers by the number of minutes that that player has played.

We decided to begin our analysis using a decision tree. Decision trees excel at dealing with noise, which would help deal with the skill gap of star players' stats when compared to average NBA players. Decision trees also partition the dataset along axis-parallel hyperplanes, which will help to classify players of the same position as they tend to record similar stats.

Next, we decided to continue our analysis by using the K-Nearest Neighbors algorithm. Of course, KNN needs data to be scaled (in order to prevent attribute domination) so we used a pipeline with a scaler. Similarly, KNN is susceptible to the curse of dimensionality, and our dataset has 20 attributes. As such, we decided to use principle component analysis in our pipeline to determine the ideal number of attributes to use in our classification.

In [21]:
def knn(features, labels):
    scaler = StandardScaler()
    pca = PCA()
    KNN = KNeighborsClassifier()
    pipeline = Pipeline([('scaler', scaler), ('PCA', pca), ('KNN', KNN)])
    param_grid = {
        'PCA__n_components': list(range(5, 21)),
        'KNN__n_neighbors': list(range(1, 15))
    }

    grid_search = GridSearchCV(pipeline, param_grid, cv=5, scoring='accuracy')
    grid_search.fit(features, labels)

    print('Best params:', grid_search.best_params_)
    print('Accuracy:', grid_search.best_score_ * 100)
    
    return grid_search

# for use in our voting ensemble
clf1 = knn(features, labels)

Next up is Naive Bayes. Contrary to KNN, Bayesian classification does not suffer from the curse of dimensionality, meaning we will not have to use PCA to select features for classification. However, Naive Bayes assumes that all features are independent of one another. With basketball, we know this is not the case (players with more assists will take fewer shots and have more turnovers, players with more 2-pointers will take fewer 3-pointers, etc.).

Next up is Support Vector Machines. SVM creates linear decision boundaries by transforming nonlinear data. As such, we needed to use a pipeline with a scaler and PCA to determine the best number of attributes to use (similar to our KNN analysis above).

In [23]:
def svm(features, labels):
    scaler = StandardScaler()
    pca = PCA()
    svc = SVC(gamma='scale')
    pipeline = Pipeline([('scaler', scaler), ('PCA', pca), ('SVC', svc)])
    param_grid = {
        'PCA__n_components': list(range(5, 21)),
        'SVC__kernel': ['linear', 'rbf', 'poly']
    }

    grid_search = GridSearchCV(pipeline, param_grid, cv=5, scoring='accuracy')

    nested_score = cross_val_score(grid_search, features, labels, cv=10)
    print('Accuracy:', nested_score.mean() * 100)
    
    return grid_search

# for use in our voting ensemble
clf3 = svm(features, labels)

Prior to ensembling classifiers together, we decided to use neural network classifiers. Neural networks excel at fitting to complex decision boundaries, which is both a blessing and a curse (can cause overfitting). However, neural networks also excel at identifying irrelevant attributes, which eliminates the need to use PCA in a pipeline for our analysis. However, we have to scale the data to prevent attribute domination.

Because of the success of the single decision tree, we decided to also try using a random forest classifier. A random forest classifier selects a subset of the attributes for splitting as opposed to examining all the attributes of the dataset. Although decision trees are not susceptible to the curse of dimensionality, we expected this to yield improved results over the single decision tree as we are essentially creating bagged decision trees trained on different attributes.

As the final step in our analysis, we decided to implement a voting ensemble classifier using the best classifiers from each of our 6 previous classification algorithms. Voting classifiers tend to perform better than each individual classifiers when all the classifiers have similar accuracies. Since all of our algorithms fall in the 60% range, voting classifiers seemed perfect for our final classification algorithm.

In [26]:
def voting_ensemble(features, labels, clfs):
    eclf = VotingClassifier(estimators=[('ddt', clfs[0]), ('knn', clfs[1]), ('nb', clfs[2]), 
                                       ('svm', clfs[3]), ('nn', clfs[4]), ('rf', clfs[5])], voting='hard')

    results = cross_val_predict(eclf, features, labels, cv=10)
    mat = confusion_matrix(labels, results)
    
    accuracy = 0
    for i in range(5):
        accuracy += mat[i][i]
    accuracy /= len(labels)
    accuracy *= 100
    
    print('Accuracy:', accuracy)
    
    return results

results = voting_ensemble(features, labels, [clf0, clf1, clf2, clf3, clf4, clf5])

From the classification report, the first thing that stands out is the high precision and recall of point guards. Compared to values around 0.6 for the other positions, point gaurd has a value greater than 0.8. We can trace this back to an earlier bar graph we plotted. There, it's clear that point guards have much greater assists. This would make it easier to differentiate and correctly classify PG.

Another interesting observation from the classifcation report is the low precision and recall of both forward positions, power forward and small forward. Both of these positions have precision and recall less than 0.6. This can be due to the fact that these positions normally are not the biggest men on the court nor the smallest/quickest. Having being the average size this allows the position to have the most versatility in how the player is used, each team using that unique players skillset. This results in a lack of distinct features to differentiate from other positions, and having the lowest precision and recall.

The confusion matrix above has predicted results on the X-axis and actual label on the Y-axis. This matrix is an incredible representation of the overlap between positions in the NBA. Two of the most glaring examples is between the guard positions and the power forward/center positions. In our case, 864 actual power forwards were classified as centers. On the other hand, 678 actual centers were classified as power forwards. This leads to the somewhat mediocre precision and recall values for the power forward position. Likewise, the overlap between the two guard positions is just as obvious: 453 actual shooting guards were classified as point guards, and 300 actual point guards were classified as shooting guards.

Further Exploration¶

Many NBA experts have argued that since the inception of the 3-point line, the five traditional positions have become irrelevant. Instead, there are only guards (PG, SG) and fowards (SF, PF, C) or backcourt and frontcourt, respectively. They argue that the distinction between guard positions and forward positions is too ambigious; traditional guards often share ball handling responsibilities and forwards' skill sets overlap. Because of this new wave of thought, we wanted to explore a narrowed classification problem: classifying players as either guards (G) or forwards (F).

This problem is relatively straightforward. We will still use the same algorithms presented in our original analysis, only classifying records to a smaller labelspace. The first task is to map all PGs and SGs to 'G' and SFs, PFs, and Cs to 'F'.

Conclusion¶

When we began our exploration of NBA box-score statistics, we set out to determine if a player's position could be determined by their season's statistics. Our initial analysis was somewhat successful: our best model (the voting classifier) had an accuracy of 67%. However, it also pointed out some of the flaws in modern basketball position analysis: the distinction between positions is sometimes ambiguous.

It is important to note that the position a player is listed at throughout the season is ultimately up to the team's coach to decide. That position may not be the role they actually serve on the team. For example, a player may be listed as a shooting guard for the season yet be filling the role of the team's point guard.

When reducing the labels to just guards and forwards, the efficacy of our classification analysis increased dramatically. With ~90% accuracy across the board, the algorithms were able to identify guards and forwards with much greater accuracy than our previous exploration. This confirms our suspicions: the overlap between backcourt positions and frontcourt positions is ambigious; the responsibilities shared by point guards and shooting guards are so similar that players could often be listed at either position throughout the season. Similarly, the roles of the frontcourt (SF, PF, and C) blur the individual distinctions of each of the three traditional big men positions.

Glossary¶

Descriptions taken from basketball reference¶

  1. #: Unique identifier for each record
  2. SeasonStart: year the season described in that record began
  3. PlayerName: name of the player whose stats are listed in this record
  4. Pos: Position; position this player played at this season
  5. Age: Age of the player during that season
  6. Tm: Team; the team the player played on during that season
  7. G: Games; number of games played in by that player during that season
  8. GS: Games started; number of games started by that player during that season (member of the first lineup to play in that game)
  9. MP: Minutes played; number of minutes played during that season
  10. PER: Player efficiency rating; per-minute rating of a player's performance
  11. TS%: True shooting percentage; a measure of shooting efficiency that takes into account field goals, 3-point field goals, and free throws
  12. 3PAr: 3-point attempt rate; 3PA / FGA
  13. FTr: Free throw rate; FTA/FGA
  14. ORB%: Offensive rebound percentage; an estimate of the percentage of available offensive rebounds a player grabbed while he was on the floor
  15. DRB%: Defensive rebound percentage; an estimate of the percentage of available defensive rebounds a player grabbed while he was on the floor
  16. TRB%: Total rebound percentage; an estimate of the percentage of available rebounds a player grabbed while he was on the floor
  17. AST%: Assist percentage; an estimate of the percentage of teammate field goals a player assisted while he was on the floor
  18. STL%: Steal percentage; an estimate of the percentage of opponent possessions that end with a steal by the player while he was on the floor
  19. BLK%: Block percentage; an estimate of the percentage of opponent two-point field goal attempts blocked by the player while he was on the floor
  20. TOV%: Turnover percentage; an estimate of turnovers per 100 plays
  21. USG%: Usage percentage; an estimate of the percentage of team plays used by a player while he was on the floor
  22. blanl: a placeholder attribute created by the aggregator of the dataset
  23. OWS: Offensive win shares; a metric designed to estimate the number of wins a player contributes with their offensive contributions
  24. DWS: Defensive win shares; a metric designed to estimate the number of wins a player contributes with their defensive contributions
  25. WS: Win shares; a metric designed to estimate the number of wins a player contributes with their offensive and defensive contributions
  26. WS/48: Win shares per 48; a metric designed to estimate the number of wins a player contributes, scaled to one game
  27. blank2: another placeholder attribute created by the aggregator of the dataset
  28. OBPM: Offensive box plus-minus; box score-based metric that estimates a basketball players offensive contribution to the team when that player is on the court
  29. DBPM: Defensive box plus-minus; box score-based metric that estimates a basketball players defensive contribution to the team when that player is on the court
  30. BPM: Box plus-minus; box score-based metric that estimates a basketball players offensive contribution to the team when that player is on the court
  31. VORP: Value over replacement player; an estimate of each player's contribution to their team relative to a "replacement-level" player
  32. FG: Field goals; number of shots made by a player during the season
  33. FGA: Field goal attempts; number of shots taken by a player during the season
  34. FG%: Field goal percentage: FG / FGA
  35. 3P: 3-point field goals; number of 3-point shots made by a player during the season
  36. 3PA: 3-point field goal attempts; number of 3-point shots taken by a player during the season
  37. 3P%: 3-point field goal percentage: 3P / 3PA
  38. 2P: 2-point field goals; number of 2-point shots made by a player during the season
  39. 2PA: 2-point field goal attempts; number of 2-point shots taken by a player during the season
  40. 2P%: 2-point field goal percentage: 2P / 2PA
  41. eFG%: Effective field-goal percentage; (FG + .5 * 3P) / FGA; a shooting percentage metric that weights 3-point shots greater than 2-point shots
  42. FT: Free throws; number of free throw shots made by a player during the season
  43. FTA: Free throw attempts; number of free throw shots taken by a player during the season
  44. FT%: Free throw percentage: FT / FTA
  45. ORB: Offensive rebounds; rebounds earned while the player's team has possession of the ball
  46. DRB: Defensive rebounds; rebounds earned while the opponent's team has possession of the ball
  47. TRB: Total rebounds; ORB + DRB
  48. AST: Assists
  49. STL: Steals
  50. BLK: Blocks
  51. TOV: Turnovers
  52. PF: Personal fouls
  53. PTS: Points; 3 3P + 2 2P + FT
  54. PlayerSalary: Amount of money paid to this player for that season
